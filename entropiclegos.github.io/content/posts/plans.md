## What I plan to write about
I think going forward I'll leave aside my previous quest for figuring out the physical meaning (if any) of QM for a while and write more about fun things that don't, in my experience, receive enough attention in the physics-blogging world. Here's a rough list of topics I want to cover over the next few months.

1. Einstein's original photon paper. Most texts and other forms of expository content don't highlight the sheer brilliance of Einstein's original photon paper where he, by using tools from statmech, demonstrated that the Wien (high-energy) regime of the black-body radiation curve implies that EM radiation behaves like an ideal gas. They just give the derivation of Planck's formula while mentioning in passing that Planck's ad hoc quantisation was interpreted by Einstein as light being of particulate nature, but that makes it seem like Einstein just plucked this out of thin air. He didn't. (Or maybe he did, since thin air is reasonably well-approximated as an ideal gas?)
2. Fermi-Dirac statistics and its application to astrophysical objects and solid state physics. Fermi-Dirac statistics single-handedly explains much of what we regard as the real world and I think that it just doesn't receive enough love. In particular, I plan on doing a few posts on say when a lump of rock turns spherical, the stability of astrophysical objects (which would in part involve Bose-Einstein statistics for a main sequence star but I digress), compact or otherwise, by what's called degeneracy pressure, where the Chandrasekhar limit comes from and so on.
3. Quantum Hall effect and topological states of matter. Who knew stretchy gooey maths could have real-world applications?
4. I think I want to do a few where I talk about QFT and GR from a mathematical standpoint, but these will need more research, which by the way is part of the reason why I want to talk about these - I want for example to be absolutely clear when I say that an elementary particle is an irreducible representation of the Lorentz group. 
5. Over on Facebook, I've started a series on deriving (nearly) all of physics from Lorentz invariance and path integrals, and I'd like adapting that to a format suitable for posting here, which means it would probably be a more condensed and mathematically advanced treatment.
6. Cosmology. I realise that this isn't very specific, but the field receives more than its fair share of the spotlight so it's hard to pick something other people haven't already explained a thousand times better than me before. Right now I'm thinking the CMB since it contains information etched into it on several levels and I find that breathtaking. 
7. The cosmic distance ladder. Never grows old. Suitable for ages 8-80. It's mesmerising. Awe-inspiring. I don't have enough adjectives.

That's the rough plan. I might also start a series on hidden gems from classics such as the Principia and the Feynman Lectures (am I the first one to label this a classic? Hope not!). Big goals, small man, hoping for the best!
